# All Carriers Allowed: Design Principles for Distributed Ledger-Based Cross-Domain Data Sharing Between Guarantee of Origin Schemes

## Abstract

Guarantees of origin (GOs) are the principal instrument for tracking renewable energy attributes across European energy markets. As the revised Renewable Energy Directive (RED III) mandates GO schemes for alternative energy carriers—hydrogen, biogas, and district heating—interoperability between previously siloed registries becomes an operational necessity. Energy carrier conversion processes, such as power-to-hydrogen electrolysis, require cross-domain data sharing between GO schemes that current centralized IT architectures cannot adequately support. This paper employs a Design Science Research (DSR) approach to derive design principles (DPs) for interoperable GO scheme architectures. We instantiate these DPs in a Hyperledger Fabric prototype, iteratively developed over eight design cycles, and evaluate the artifact against four meta-requirements: scalability, verifiability, privacy, and interoperability. Caliper benchmarks on a production-grade deployment demonstrate sustained 50 TPS write throughput and 2,000 TPS read throughput, confirmed across both the single-channel (v7.0) and multi-channel (v8.0) architectures. The eighth design cycle introduces a multi-channel architecture that partitions data by energy carrier and region, with a cross-channel bridge protocol enabling verifiable conversion issuance across carrier boundaries. We contribute four empirically grounded design principles to the nascent design theory for blockchain-based energy certificate systems.

**Keywords:** Guarantees of Origin; Design Science Research; Distributed Ledger Technology; Hyperledger Fabric; Energy Carrier Conversion

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Hook / Motivating Entry

In 2023, European GO markets facilitated the transfer of over 700 million certificates, each representing one megawatt-hour of renewable energy production (Association of Issuing Bodies 2023). Yet these certificates tracked only one energy carrier—electricity. With the European Commission's revised Renewable Energy Directive (RED III) mandating GO schemes for hydrogen, biogas, and district heating, the number of interacting GO registries is set to multiply. The question is no longer *whether* these registries must communicate, but *how* their underlying IT architectures can be designed to support verifiable, privacy-preserving, cross-domain data sharing at scale.

### 1.2 Problem Relevance

The decarbonization of the European energy system depends critically on sector coupling—the integration of electricity, gas, hydrogen, and heating grids through energy carrier conversion technologies such as electrolysis, methanation, and combined heat and power. When a producer converts electricity into green hydrogen via electrolysis, the renewable energy attributes documented by electricity GOs must be verifiably transferred onto the resulting hydrogen GOs. This process, termed *conversion issuance* in the CEN-EN 16325 standard, requires cross-domain data sharing between GO registries that are currently operated by different national bodies using heterogeneous centralized IT systems (Verwimp et al. 2022).

Current GO scheme architectures suffer from several structural deficiencies that are exacerbated by cross-carrier interoperability requirements: high transaction costs from monopolistic platform operators (Bogensperger and Zeiselmair 2020), long GO approval cycles driven by manual auditing processes (Nofuentes et al. 2022), lack of transparency to market participants enabling potential abuse (Li et al. 2022), and privacy concerns from centralized data storage with single points of failure (Albrecht et al. 2018). These problems have been individually documented in the literature, but their interaction in the context of cross-carrier conversion issuance creates a design challenge that has received minimal systematic attention from IS researchers.

### 1.3 Research Gap

While a substantial body of Green IS literature has investigated the general suitability of distributed ledger technology (DLT) for energy GO schemes (Sedlmeir et al. 2021; Knirsch et al. 2018; Al Sadawi et al. 2021), no prior work has systematically derived design principles for GO architectures that must support cross-domain data sharing between multiple energy carrier schemes. Existing DLT-based GO prototypes address single-carrier electricity certificates (Ølnes et al. 2022) or provide conceptual frameworks without rigorous evaluation against production-scale benchmarks (Chuang et al. 2019). The few studies addressing multi-carrier energy certificate systems remain at the conceptual level (Mould et al. 2022; Körner 2021), without translating architectural requirements into actionable design guidance grounded in implemented and evaluated artifacts. Moreover, existing IS design theory lacks prescriptive knowledge for the specific technical challenges arising from conversion issuance: verifying energy carrier conversion ratios across registry boundaries, preventing cross-carrier double counting of renewable attributes, and preserving data sovereignty when commercially sensitive production data must flow between organizations. The conversion issuance use case—requiring simultaneous verifiable data sharing across scheme boundaries, privacy preservation of commercially sensitive production data, and scalable throughput for IoT-integrated metering devices—remains an open design problem at the intersection of energy informatics and IS design theory.

### 1.4 Research Question

**RQ:** *How to design an information system of interoperable GO schemes that allows verifiable cross-domain data sharing and asset transfer to enable energy carrier conversion processes in multi-carrier energy systems?*

### 1.5 Contributions

This paper makes three contributions:

(1) **Theoretical contribution:** We derive four empirically grounded design principles—scalability, verifiability, privacy, and interoperability—for DLT-based GO scheme architectures, formulated using the anatomy of a design principle schema (Gregor et al. 2020). These DPs extend the nascent design theory for blockchain-based energy certificate systems by addressing the previously unexamined cross-carrier interoperability dimension.

(2) **Practical contribution:** We provide a fully functional Hyperledger Fabric prototype (10 smart contract namespaces, ~50 exported functions), iteratively developed over eight design cycles with quantitative performance evidence from Caliper benchmarks. The eighth cycle implements a multi-channel architecture with carrier-specific channels and a cross-channel bridge protocol for conversion issuance.

(3) **Methodological contribution:** We demonstrate how iterative DSR cycles can be structured around documented architecture decisions, with each cycle addressing specific gaps identified through systematic architecture critiques and performance benchmarking. The eight-cycle process—spanning conceptual design, six prototyping iterations, and a multi-channel architecture improvement—provides a template for rigorous artifact development in IS research.

### 1.6 Paper Structure

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 provides the conceptual background on multi-carrier energy systems, GO scheme architectures, and DLT components relevant to the design problem. Section 3 describes our DSR approach, including the justification for DSR, the iterative design cycle methodology embracing eight cycles from conceptual design through multi-channel implementation, and the concurrent evaluation strategy combining qualitative and quantitative methods. Section 4 presents the results: challenges identified through a multi-vocal literature review, meta-requirements derived from the problem space, four design principles formulated using the Gregor et al. (2020) anatomy schema, the conceptual architecture, the Hyperledger Fabric v7.0 single-channel prototype, and the v8.0 multi-channel architecture. Section 5 evaluates the artifact against the design principles using Caliper performance benchmarks and systematic architecture critiques. Section 6 discusses findings in relation to existing literature, positions the contribution as a nascent design theory, and outlines practical implications for GO registry operators. Section 7 concludes with a summary, limitations, and directions for future research.

## 2. Conceptual Background

### 2.1 Multi-Carrier Energy Systems and GO Schemes

The complete decarbonization of the economy will chiefly rely on electrification; however, alternative energy carriers such as green hydrogen, biogas, and thermal energy will also play a significant role in sectors that are difficult to electrify directly—heavy industry, long-haul transport, and high-temperature heating (Heffron et al. 2020). The integration of multiple energy carriers through conversion technologies is recognized as essential for closing the flexibility gap of intermittent renewable energy generation (Fridgen et al. 2020). Power-to-hydrogen electrolysis provides storage flexibility by converting surplus renewable electricity into storable hydrogen, while power-to-gas methanation and combined heat and power plants offer inter-sectoral transmission flexibility across spatial and temporal boundaries (Brown et al. 2018). These sector coupling technologies create *multi-carrier energy systems* where energy flows across previously isolated domains—electricity, gas, hydrogen, and heating grids.

The RED III directive mandates that all EU member states establish GO schemes for these alternative energy carriers by 2024, creating an unprecedented interoperability challenge for registry architectures that were designed for single-carrier electricity tracking. GOs are the principal instrument for tracking renewable energy attributes under a book-and-claim chain of custody model, where the physical energy flow is decoupled from the attribute claim (AIB 2021). A GO documents the energy source, production method, temporal and spatial characteristics, environmental attributes, and subsidy status of one MWh of energy production. The European Association of Issuing Bodies (AIB) coordinates cross-border electricity GO transfers through its centralized Hub, processing over 700 million certificates annually across 26 member countries.

The CEN-EN 16325 standard defines GO data fields, lifecycle processes (issuance, transfer, cancellation, expiry), and cross-border exchange protocols. Its 2021 revision introduced the critical concept of *conversion issuance*: "the production of an Energy Carrier from one or more Inputs including at least one other Energy Carrier" (van Stein-Callenfels et al. 2021). Conversion issuance requires that renewable attributes from input GOs are verifiably transferred to output GOs—accounting for conversion efficiency losses (e.g., 60–70% for electrolysis), emission inheritance from the input carrier, and subsidy carry-over restrictions. This process demands cross-domain data sharing between the input carrier's GO registry and the output carrier's GO registry, which may be operated by different national bodies with heterogeneous IT systems.

Three stakeholder groups participate in GO schemes: issuing bodies (registry operators, typically transmission system operators or designated national authorities), producers (energy generators with registered metering devices), and consumers (energy buyers who cancel GOs to make renewable energy claims). Cross-carrier interoperability adds a fourth interaction dimension: coordination between issuing bodies of different energy carrier registries, a function currently performed by the AIB Hub for electricity GOs but not yet established for hydrogen, biogas, or thermal energy carriers.

### 2.2 Distributed Ledger Technology for Energy Certificate Systems

DLT provides a timestamped, append-only shared database maintained by multiple nodes without requiring a trusted central operator (Androulaki et al. 2018). For GO schemes, DLT offers several architecturally relevant properties: tamper-evident history through cryptographic linking of blocks provides an immutable audit trail; multi-party consensus without a single platform operator reduces dependence on monopolistic intermediaries; programmable business logic via smart contracts enables automated GO lifecycle management; and selective data sharing through private data collections in permissioned networks addresses privacy requirements without sacrificing verifiability.

The blockchain suitability literature provides structured decision frameworks for evaluating DLT applicability (Gramlich et al. 2023; Wüst and Gervais 2018). Applied to GO schemes, the decision criteria are met: persistent state storage is required (GO lifecycle records), multiple mutually distrustful writers exist (issuing bodies across jurisdictions), a trusted third party cannot serve all participants equally across 27 EU member states (regulatory sovereignty constraints), all participants are known (permissioned network of national authorities), and public verifiability of cancellation statements is desired (consumer protection and greenwashing prevention).

Hyperledger Fabric is a permissioned blockchain framework designed for enterprise consortium networks, distinguishing it from public blockchain platforms through its identity-aware architecture. Key architectural features relevant to GO schemes include: (a) an execute-order-validate (EOV) transaction flow that enables deterministic smart contract execution with concurrent endorsement by multiple organizations; (b) organizations defined by Membership Service Providers (MSPs) with X.509 certificate-based identity management, enabling fine-grained access control; (c) private data collections (PDCs) allowing confidential data sharing between designated subsets of organizations through gossip-based peer-to-peer dissemination; (d) modular consensus via pluggable ordering services, with Raft providing crash fault tolerance for production deployments; and (e) a channel architecture providing logical network isolation for data partitioning across jurisdictions. The CouchDB state database enables JSON-based rich queries against the world state, supporting the complex query patterns required for GO registry operations such as filtering by energy source, production period, and lifecycle status.

Prior DLT-based GO prototypes have demonstrated the general feasibility of blockchain for energy certification (Chuang et al. 2019; Ølnes et al. 2022), but have focused exclusively on single-carrier electricity certificates. The multi-carrier conversion issuance use case introduces additional requirements—cross-domain data sharing, conversion efficiency verification, and multi-registry coordination—that have not been addressed in existing prototype implementations.

### 2.3 Key Constructs and Relationships

The conceptual model connecting the problem domain to the solution artifact is structured around four key constructs and their relationships. First, **multi-carrier energy systems** generate the need for cross-domain data sharing through energy carrier conversion processes. The physical act of converting electricity to hydrogen (or gas, or heat) creates a dependency between the input carrier's GO registry and the output carrier's GO registry that cannot be satisfied by isolated centralized systems. Second, **GO scheme architectures** must satisfy design requirements derived from the conversion issuance use case. These requirements span multiple quality dimensions—scalability for high-frequency IoT metering data, verifiability for regulatory compliance, privacy for commercial data protection, and interoperability for cross-carrier coordination—creating a multi-objective optimization problem. Third, **design principles** prescribe how DLT-based architectures should be structured to meet these requirements. Formulated at a level of abstraction above any specific platform, DPs provide reusable design guidance that is transferable across implementations (Iivari et al. 2021). Fourth, **Hyperledger Fabric artifacts** instantiate the design principles in a concrete, evaluable prototype, providing empirical evidence for the DPs through measurable performance and architectural characteristics.

The mapping between problem space and solution space follows the DSR framework of Hevner et al. (2004): challenges identified through literature review map to meta-requirements, which are addressed by design principles, which are instantiated in the prototype artifact. The evaluation cycle assesses whether the artifact satisfies the meta-requirements, feeding improvements back into subsequent design iterations. This iterative build-evaluate cycle (Sonnenberg and vom Brocke 2012) was executed eight times over three phases: three cycles during a master thesis (conceptual design, initial prototype, refined prototype), four subsequent cycles extending the artifact to production-grade maturity (v4.0–v7.0), and an eighth cycle extending the architecture to multi-channel operation (v8.0).

## 3. Research Approach

### 3.1 Design Science Research Approach

We follow the DSR methodology of Peffers et al. (2007), structured within the conceptual framework of Hevner et al. (2004) and its three-cycle view (Hevner 2007). The research addresses a wicked design problem—how to architect interoperable GO schemes—where no algorithmic solution exists and the problem space is defined by multiple conflicting requirements (scalability vs. privacy, verifiability vs. confidentiality). DSR is the appropriate methodological choice because the research objective is an *IT artifact* (a GO scheme architecture and its HLF instantiation) rather than a behavioral theory, and the primary contribution is *prescriptive* knowledge (design principles) rather than *descriptive* or *explanatory* knowledge.

The DSR framework relates the design process to three cycles: the *relevance cycle* connecting the research to the environment (GO market stakeholders, regulatory requirements such as RED III and CEN-EN 16325, existing registry architectures operated by national issuing bodies), the *rigor cycle* grounding the research in the knowledge base (DLT theory, cryptographic primitives, permissioned blockchain architectural patterns, energy informatics literature), and the *design cycle* iterating between artifact construction and evaluation. Following Hevner et al.'s (2004) seven guidelines, the research: (1) produces a viable artifact (the HLF prototype), (2) addresses a relevant problem (cross-carrier GO interoperability mandated by RED III), (3) rigorously evaluates the artifact (Caliper benchmarks, architecture critiques), (4) provides clear contributions (four design principles formulated as nascent design theory), (5) applies rigorous methods (multi-vocal literature review, quantitative benchmarking), (6) searches the design space iteratively (eight design cycles with 30+ documented architecture decisions), and (7) communicates results to both technical and managerial audiences.

Design principles are formulated using the anatomy schema of Gregor et al. (2020), which specifies four components: the *aim* (what the DP seeks to achieve), the *context* (under which conditions it applies), the *mechanism* (how it achieves the aim), and the *rationale* (why the mechanism works in this context). This schema ensures that DPs are articulated at an appropriate level of abstraction—specific enough to provide actionable guidance, yet general enough to be reusable beyond the specific Hyperledger Fabric instantiation. Reusability is assessed against the minimum reusability criteria of Iivari et al. (2021), requiring that each DP can be applied in at least one additional context beyond the original design.

### 3.2 Iterative Software Design

The artifact was developed over eight design cycles spanning three distinct phases. Cycle 1 comprised conceptual architecture design: a multi-vocal literature review (32 academic and 12 grey literature sources across four databases) identified eight challenges, which were mapped to four design requirements. The blockchain suitability decision framework of Gramlich et al. (2023) confirmed DLT applicability. Cycle 2 comprised prototypical implementation: basic GO lifecycle functions (issuance, transfer, conversion, cancellation) in Go chaincode deployed to a four-organization Hyperledger Fabric 2.5 network on a GCP virtual machine. Cycle 3 refined the prototype with additional features (cancellation statements, consumption declarations, expiry checking) and updated the conceptual architecture based on iterative refinement.

The subsequent four cycles (v4.0–v7.0) extended the prototype into a production-grade platform, each structured around documented *architecture decisions* that record the problem statement, considered alternatives, chosen approach, and consequences—providing an auditable decision trail for design choices. v4.0 addressed scalability and privacy: cursor-based pagination replaced full-scan list queries, tombstone patterns replaced hard deletes, timestamp drift guards prevented backdated issuance, hash commitment schemes enabled selective quantity verification, and CouchDB retention policies bounded private data storage growth. v5.0 addressed interoperability: CEN-EN 16325 data model alignment standardized GO fields, API versioning enabled client compatibility checks, dynamic organization onboarding supported consortium growth, biogas carrier support demonstrated multi-carrier extensibility, and CQRS event-driven architecture decoupled read and write paths. v6.0 hardened the system with cryptographically secure 128-bit commitment salts replacing transaction-ID-derived salts, comprehensive CEN-EN 16325 field validation at write time, state-based per-asset endorsement policies binding modification rights to asset owners, and a formal deprecation lifecycle for legacy API endpoints. v7.0 integrated market features: a cross-registry bridge protocol with an explicit export/confirm/import state machine, ECDSA P-256 smart meter attestation for hardware-rooted metering trust, and an external plausibility oracle ingesting ENTSO-E grid generation data for automated production cross-referencing.

The third phase (v8.0) addressed the scalability and privacy limitations identified in the v7.0 architecture critique—principally, the single-channel full-replication problem and the bridge contract's lack of true cross-channel semantics. v8.0 extends the network topology from a single shared channel to a channel-per-carrier architecture, introduces a cross-channel bridge relay protocol (Lock → Mint → Finalize), and reconfigures organizational membership so that participants join only the channels relevant to their energy carrier.

Each cycle concluded with concurrent evaluation (Sonnenberg and vom Brocke 2012): quantitative assessment via Hyperledger Caliper v0.6.0 performance benchmarks (10 workers, fixed TPS send rates, measurement of throughput, latency, and failure rates) on a production-grade Hetzner VM (16 vCPU, 32 GB RAM, Ubuntu 24.04), and qualitative assessment via systematic architecture critiques evaluating scalability, privacy, verifiability, and interoperability through "arguments in favour" and "arguments against" analysis. Gap identification from each critique directly informed the architecture decisions for the subsequent cycle, creating a traceable chain from identified weakness to implemented solution.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Challenges from the Problem Space

A multi-vocal literature review—analyzing 32 academic sources and 12 grey literature sources across four databases (IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, AIS eLibrary, Google Scholar)—identified eight challenges (C1–C8) preventing cross-carrier interoperability of GO schemes. The challenges were coded following the Gioia et al. (2013) methodology, progressing from first-order informant terms through second-order theoretical themes to aggregate dimensions corresponding to the four meta-requirements.

| ID | Challenge | Description | Sources |
|----|-----------|-------------|---------|
| C1 | High transaction costs | Monopolistic platform operators charge fees that inhibit high-frequency transactions | Bogensperger and Zeiselmair 2020; Hulshof et al. 2019 |
| C2 | Manual auditing cycles | GO approval processes require manual verification, creating multi-week delays | Nofuentes et al. 2022; Zhang et al. 2023 |
| C3 | Unreliable data sources | Alternative carrier schemes lack standardized metering and data quality assurance | Sailer et al. 2021 |
| C4 | Transparency deficit | Opaque registry operations enable potential double counting within a single carrier | Li et al. 2022; Ølnes et al. 2022 |
| C5 | Cross-carrier double counting | Attribute duplication during conversion processes when registries do not coordinate | Sailer et al. 2021; Verwimp et al. 2022 |
| C6 | Privacy invasion | Centralized databases expose commercially sensitive production data to all participants | Albrecht et al. 2018 |
| C7 | Single point of failure | Centralized architectures create availability and integrity risks | Knirsch et al. 2018 |
| C8 | Low harmonization | Carrier-specific registries use incompatible data models and exchange protocols | Wang et al. 2019 |

### 4.2 Meta-Requirements

From the challenge mapping, four meta-requirements (MRs) were derived:

**MR1: Scalability** — The system must sustain the transaction throughput of national GO markets (thousands of metering devices reporting at 15-minute intervals) while minimizing transaction costs and automating approval cycles. *(Addresses C1, C2, C3)*

**MR2: Verifiability** — The system must provide cryptographically verifiable provenance from physical metering devices to issued GOs, transparent lifecycle tracking visible to all participants, and resistance to double counting across energy carriers. *(Addresses C3, C4, C5)*

**MR3: Privacy** — The system must protect commercially sensitive production data (quantities, emissions, production methods) from unauthorized parties while enabling selective disclosure to authorized verifiers, without compromising the verifiability of public GO attributes. *(Addresses C6, C7)*

**MR4: Interoperability** — The system must support data sharing and asset transfer between GO schemes of different energy carriers, conform to the CEN-EN 16325 standard for semantic compatibility, and enable cross-border exchange through standardized protocols. *(Addresses C5, C8)*

**DP1 elaboration.** The channel-partitioned scalability principle addresses the fundamental limitation of single-channel permissioned blockchain architectures: every peer must replicate the complete world state. In the v7.0 single-channel design, all four organizations share one Fabric channel, forcing every peer to store all electricity GOs, all hydrogen GOs, and all biogas GOs regardless of the peer's organizational role. Extrapolated to the full European GO market—27 member states with multiple carriers—this would require each peer to store approximately 9.8 TB/year (Albrecht 2024), rendering gossip-based replication unsustainable.

The v8.0 multi-channel architecture addresses this by assigning each energy carrier within a region its own Fabric channel (e.g., `electricity-de`, `hydrogen-de`). Each channel has an independent Raft consensus pipeline, world state database, and endorsement context. Electricity GO issuance no longer contends with hydrogen GO transfers for ordering slots. The aggregate platform throughput scales linearly with channel count: with two channels, the system supports 100 TPS aggregate; with 27 national channels per carrier, the theoretical aggregate reaches 1,350 TPS.

As a side note, an early prototype (v1.0) used a shared counter for sequential GO identifier generation, which caused Multi-Version Concurrency Control (MVCC) read conflicts and reduced throughput to 0.8 TPS. Replacing the counter with transaction-hash-derived identifiers resolved this immediately. While instructive as a lesson about Fabric's concurrency model, this was a straightforward implementation correction rather than a fundamental design insight—the real scalability challenge lies in the channel topology and data partitioning decisions that v8.0 addresses.

Cursor-based pagination complements the channel architecture by bounding read-path latency. With 901 registered devices, paginated queries maintain 10ms latency at 500 TPS, while unpaginated equivalents take 15.3 seconds—a 1,533× difference that validates pagination as essential for any dataset beyond trivial size.

**DP2 elaboration.** Meter-to-ledger verifiability addresses the fundamental trust deficit in energy production data. In current GO schemes, producers submit production declarations that issuing bodies accept largely at face value, with only periodic physical audits. The prototype establishes a verifiability architecture where **smart meters serve as the data oracles**—the authoritative, hardware-rooted sources of production data.

The trust architecture works as follows. Smart meters at production sites are registered on-chain with their ECDSA P-256 public keys and organizational affiliation. When a meter records energy production, it signs the reading (timestamp, quantity, device identifier) with its private key. The `SubmitSignedReading` chaincode function accepts the reading payload and its ECDSA signature, retrieves the registered public key from the world state, and cryptographically verifies the signature before persisting the reading. This mechanism ensures that every on-chain production record traces back to a specific physical device through an unforgeable cryptographic link—the smart meter *is* the oracle.

Independently, the system ingests grid generation data from the ENTSO-E Transparency Platform as a **plausibility cross-reference**. This data—recording aggregate generation output by energy source, timestamp, and installed capacity for each bidding zone—enables automated sanity checks: a GO claiming 500 MWh of solar production during nighttime hours, or wind production during a documented calm period, would be flagged for review. The grid data does not replace the smart meter as the authoritative data source; rather, it provides an independent second opinion that can detect implausible claims. The `CrossReferenceGO` function compares a GO's claimed production period and energy source against the stored grid records and returns a match/mismatch assessment.

The v8.0 multi-channel bridge adds a further verification dimension: for every cross-channel conversion, the source channel records a `CrossChannelLock` and the destination channel records a `BridgeMint`, both linked by a cryptographic lock receipt hash. An auditor with access to both channels can independently verify the complete conversion chain—from the smart meter's signed reading through issuance, lifecycle tracking, and cross-channel bridge attestation. This dual-ledger audit trail is structurally similar to double-entry accounting: the lock (debit) on one channel must match the mint (credit) on the other.

**DP3 elaboration.** The selective disclosure principle resolves the tension between the transparency required for regulatory auditing and the confidentiality demanded by market participants for commercially sensitive production data. The v8.0 architecture combines three orthogonal privacy mechanisms that provide defense in depth.

First, **channel-level isolation** ensures that organizations only see data for carriers they participate in. An electricity producer's peer has no membership on the `hydrogen-de` channel and therefore has zero visibility into hydrogen GOs—not even their existence or transaction patterns. This is structural privacy enforced by Fabric's channel architecture, not application-level access control.

Second, within each channel, the GO data model is **split into public and private components**. The public struct carries identity, lifecycle status, and CEN-EN 16325 metadata; the private struct carries quantities, emissions, production methods, and commitment salts, stored in organization-scoped private data collections accessible only to designated MSPs.

Third, **hash commitments** bridge the public and private components. The public ledger stores `SHA-256(quantity || 128-bit random salt)`, enabling a verifier to confirm a claimed quantity by recomputing the hash with the disclosed salt—without the quantity being visible on the public ledger. The upgrade from transaction-ID-derived salts to 128-bit `crypto/rand` salts ensures the commitment is computationally secure ($2^{128}$ search space).

Per-asset endorsement policies add a fourth layer: after creation, each GO's endorsement policy is bound to the producing organization and issuer, preventing unauthorized modifications even by a majority coalition of other channel members.

**DP4 elaboration.** Standards-aligned extensibility anticipates a regulatory environment that is still forming. By aligning the data model with the CEN-EN 16325 revision (which defines fields for all energy carriers), the architecture ensures that GOs for future carriers can be created without data model modifications. Write-time validation enforces ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 country codes, EECS energy source code patterns, enumerated support scheme categories, and production period reasonableness checks, making every on-chain GO structurally export-ready.

The v8.0 cross-channel bridge protocol transforms interoperability from passive record-keeping to active conversion issuance. The three-phase Lock → Mint → Finalize state machine enables a GO on one channel to be locked, its renewable attributes transferred (adjusted for conversion efficiency) to a new GO minted on another channel, and the source GO finalized as bridged. An idempotency guard (`mint_receipt_<hash>` key) prevents double-minting from the same lock.

The strongest expression of DP4 is the v8.0 extensibility model itself: adding a new energy carrier—say, thermal energy for district heating—requires provisioning a new channel, deploying a carrier-parameterized chaincode instance, and configuring organizational membership. No existing channel's configuration, chaincode, or data is modified. This is additive-only extensibility with zero regression risk.

### 4.4 Conceptual Architecture

The conceptual architecture follows a three-tier pattern adapted for distributed ledger infrastructure: a presentation layer (React frontend), an application layer (Express.js REST backend integrating the Fabric Gateway SDK for transaction submission and event listening), and a distributed ledger layer (Hyperledger Fabric 2.5 network with Raft consensus ordering).

The distributed ledger layer is organized around a tiered organization model reflecting GO market participant roles: Tier 1 organizations are *Issuers* (registry operators such as national energy authorities) with full administrative privileges including GO lifecycle management; Tier 2 organizations are *Producers* (energy generators with registered smart metering devices) authorized to submit production data and initiate issuance requests; Tier 3 organizations are *Consumers* (energy buyers) who can transfer and cancel GOs to make renewable energy claims. Each tier has distinct access privileges enforced through a combination of role-based access control (RBAC) using MSP organizational unit attributes and attribute-based access control (ABAC) at the chaincode level.

The distributed ledger layer maintains two data planes: a public world state (GO identity, lifecycle status, creation and update timestamps, CEN-EN 16325 metadata including country, energy source, grid connection point, production period, support scheme, and quantity commitment hashes) readable by all channel members, and private data collections scoped to individual organizations (production quantities in MWh, emission values in gCO2eq, production methods, device identifiers, commitment salts, and consumption declarations from conversion processes). The public plane provides the transparency and verifiability required by regulators and auditors, while the private plane preserves the commercial confidentiality demanded by market participants. This dual-plane architecture directly instantiates DP3 (Confidentiality Through Selective Disclosure).

The chaincode layer is organized into 10 independent contract namespaces grouped by functional domain: *Domain Contracts* handle core GO lifecycle operations (IssuanceContract, TransferContract, ConversionContract, CancellationContract, BiogasContract); *Market Integration Contracts* manage cross-registry communication (BridgeContract, OracleContract); *Infrastructure Contracts* provide supporting services (QueryContract, DeviceContract, AdminContract). Each namespace is independently addressable and can be upgraded without affecting others, directly instantiating the namespace-based extensibility mechanism of DP4.

### 4.5 Hyperledger Fabric Prototype (v1.0–v7.0)

The prototype was deployed on a Hetzner dedicated server (16 vCPU, 32 GB RAM, Ubuntu 24.04 LTS) running Hyperledger Fabric 2.5.12. The network comprises four organizations (issuer1MSP, eproducer1MSP for electricity producers, hproducer1MSP for hydrogen producers, buyer1MSP), each with a single peer node and CouchDB 3.3.3 state database, plus four Raft orderer nodes providing crash fault tolerance. All inter-node communication uses mutual TLS with certificates generated via `cryptogen`. The Go chaincode (`golifecycle`, compiled with Go 1.22.1) registers 10 named contract namespaces through Fabric's `contractapi.ContractInterface`, exposing approximately 50 exported transaction functions.

**Data Model.** GO assets follow the public/private split pattern prescribed by DP3. The public `GOasset` struct stores: asset ID (SHA-256 hash of the creating transaction ID), creation and update timestamps, GO type (electricity, hydrogen, biogas), producer MSP identifier, CEN-EN 16325 fields (ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 country code, EECS energy source code matching `^F\d{8}$`, EIC grid connection point code, production period start/end timestamps, enumerated support scheme category), lifecycle status (active, cancelled, transferred, expired, locked, bridged), and a quantity commitment hash (SHA-256 of quantity concatenated with a 128-bit random salt). The private `GOassetPrivate` struct stores: quantity in MWh, emissions in gCO2eq, production method description, device ID linking to the registered smart meter, the commitment salt for verification, and consumption declaration records from conversion processes.

**Smart Meter Attestation (v7.0).** The `DeviceContract` establishes hardware-rooted trust for metering data. Smart meters are registered on-chain with their ECDSA P-256 public keys, organizational affiliation, and device type. When a smart meter signs a production reading, the `SubmitSignedReading` function accepts the reading payload and its ECDSA signature, reconstructs the public key from the registered device record, and verifies the signature using Go's `crypto/ecdsa` library with the P-256 curve. Only readings with valid signatures are persisted to the world state. This positions the smart meter as the authoritative data oracle—the cryptographic root of the trust chain from physical energy production to on-chain GO issuance.

**Grid Data Cross-Referencing (v7.0).** The `OracleContract` provides a plausibility cross-reference independent of the smart meter data. Authorized operators publish grid generation records sourced from the ENTSO-E Transparency Platform, containing timestamp, energy source, installed capacity, and generation output data for each bidding zone. The `CrossReferenceGO` function compares a GO's claimed production period and energy source against stored grid records, returning a match/mismatch assessment. This serves as an automated sanity check—not a replacement for the smart meter's authoritative signed readings, but an independent plausibility layer that can flag anomalous claims (e.g., solar production during nighttime) for manual review.

**Cross-Registry Bridge (v7.0).** The initial bridge implementation managed GO movements between external registries through a three-phase state machine (export → confirm → import), mirroring the AIB Hub's EECS Inter-Domain Transfer flow. However, this operated as a record-keeping framework on the single shared channel rather than bridging between separate ledger instances. The v7.0 architecture critique identified this as a key limitation, directly motivating the v8.0 multi-channel improvement.

**CEN-EN 16325 Validation (v6.0).** Comprehensive field validation at write time enforces: ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 country codes validated against a complete enumeration, EECS energy source codes matched against the pattern `^F\d{8}$`, support scheme categories constrained to an enumerated set, EIC grid connection point codes, and production period reasonableness checks. This ensures all on-chain GOs are structurally compatible with the CEN-EN 16325 standard, making them export-ready by construction.

**Architectural Evolution.** The progression from v1.0 to v7.0 demonstrates systematic design space exploration through over 29 documented architecture decisions spanning scalability, privacy, verifiability, and interoperability. v1.0 was a monolithic chaincode that grew incrementally through six subsequent cycles: v4.0 added pagination, tombstones, and commitments; v5.0 grew from 6 to 8 contract namespaces with CEN-EN 16325 alignment and biogas support; v6.0 hardened security with cryptographic salts and per-asset endorsement; v7.0 reached 10 namespaces with the bridge, smart meter attestation, and grid data oracle. Throughout this evolution, the core write throughput of 50 TPS has remained stable—confirming that chaincode-level additions introduce negligible overhead relative to the endorsement and consensus infrastructure.

### 4.6 Multi-Channel Architecture (v8.0)

The v7.0 architecture critique identified a fundamental scalability limitation: all four organizations share a single Fabric channel, forcing every peer to replicate the complete world state for every energy carrier and every jurisdiction. Extrapolating to the full European GO market—27 member states with multiple carriers per country—this single-channel design would require each peer to store approximately 9.8 TB/year (Albrecht 2024, Data Duplication Considerations), rendering the gossip-based replication protocol unsustainable. Furthermore, the v7.0 bridge contract operated as a record-keeping framework on the same single channel rather than bridging between separate ledger instances, meaning it could not provide the isolation or atomic cross-chain guarantees required for production cross-registry transfers.

The eighth design cycle addresses these limitations through a **channel-per-carrier architecture** that leverages Hyperledger Fabric's native channel isolation mechanism to partition data by energy carrier and region.

**Channel Topology.** Each channel hosts an independent instance of the `golifecycle` chaincode parameterized for a specific carrier type. The `electricity-de` channel contains only electricity GOs and is joined by electricity-relevant organizations: the issuer (issuer1MSP), electricity producers (eproducer1MSP), and cross-carrier buyers (buyer1MSP). The `hydrogen-de` channel contains only hydrogen GOs and is joined by the issuer (issuer1MSP), hydrogen producers (hproducer1MSP), and buyers (buyer1MSP). The issuer organization spans both channels, serving as the trust anchor for cross-channel operations. A shared Raft orderer cluster serves both channels, reducing operational overhead compared to per-channel orderer deployments. peers for electricity producers no longer store hydrogen data, and vice versa, reducing per-peer storage proportionally to the number of channels the organization participates in.

**Cross-Channel Bridge Protocol.** Energy carrier conversion—the core interoperability requirement identified in C5—is implemented through a three-phase cross-channel protocol that replaces the v7.0 single-channel export/confirm/import state machine:

- **Phase 1 — LockGO** (source channel): The holder invokes `bridge:LockGO` on the `electricity-de` channel, atomically transitioning the source GO from `ACTIVE` to `LOCKED` status. The lock transaction records the target carrier, target channel, and conversion parameters (efficiency ratio, emission inheritance rules). A cryptographic lock receipt hash is computed: `SHA-256(lockID || goAssetID || sourceChannel || destinationChannel || txID)`. The locked GO cannot be transferred, cancelled, or re-locked while in this state.

- **Phase 2 — MintFromBridge** (target channel): The issuer organization, which has peer membership on both channels, reads the lock receipt from the source channel and invokes `bridge:MintFromBridge` on the `hydrogen-de` channel. This function validates the lock receipt parameters—source GO existence, locked status, conversion parameter conformance to CEN-EN 16325 efficiency and emission inheritance rules—then mints a new hydrogen GO inheriting the renewable attributes of the source GO, adjusted for the declared conversion efficiency. An idempotency guard (`mint_receipt_<lockReceiptHash>` key) ensures that each lock can produce at most one mint, eliminating the double-counting risk.

- **Phase 3 — FinalizeLock** (source channel): Upon successful minting, the issuer invokes `bridge:FinalizeLock` on the `electricity-de` channel, transitioning the source GO from `LOCKED` to `BRIDGED` status. This final state is irreversible, preventing the source GO from being unlocked or double-converted.

This Lock → Mint → Finalize pattern provides stronger atomicity guarantees than the v7.0 export/confirm/import protocol: the intermediate `LOCKED` state prevents the source GO from being used while the cross-channel operation is in progress, and the issuer's dual-channel membership provides an organizational trust root that eliminates the need for cryptographic cross-chain proofs (HTLCs or SPV). The design trades cryptographic atomicity for organizational trust—an acceptable trade-off in the permissioned GO market context where issuers are nationally designated authorities with regulatory accountability.

**Privacy Implications.** Channel-level data partitioning provides structurally stronger privacy than v7.0's private data collections within a shared channel. In v7.0, all organizations could observe public GO metadata for all carriers—including transaction patterns that revealed cross-carrier trading activity. In v8.0, a hydrogen producer's peer has no channel membership on `electricity-de` and therefore has zero visibility into electricity GOs. Channel-specific collection configs further limit private data dissemination to only the organizations on each channel.

**Scalability Implications.** The channel-per-carrier design transforms the scaling model from O(participants × carriers × regions) per peer to O(participants_per_channel) per peer. For the envisioned EU-wide deployment—27 national channels per energy carrier, with four carrier types—the per-peer storage estimate drops from 9.8 TB/year (single global channel) to approximately 725 GB/year—a 13.5× reduction within commodity hardware bounds. Aggregate throughput scales linearly with channel count, as each channel has an independent ordering pipeline.

**Extensibility Implications.** Adding a new energy carrier requires provisioning a new channel with its own genesis block, collection config, and member organizations. No existing channel's configuration, chaincode, or state is modified—true zero-impact extensibility at the infrastructure level.

The v7.0 bridge functions (`ExportGO`, `ImportGO`, `ConfirmExport`) are retained alongside the new v8.0 protocol, enabling simultaneous cross-channel bridging (for internal carrier conversion) and external registry bridging (for AIB Hub interoperability).

## 5. Evaluation

### 5.1 Evaluation Approach

The artifact was evaluated using a concurrent evaluation approach (Sonnenberg and vom Brocke 2012): quantitative Caliper performance benchmarks and systematic architecture critiques after each design cycle. The architecture critique methodology analyzes each of the four design principles through "arguments in favour" (evidence supporting the DP) and "arguments against" (identified gaps and limitations), ensuring a balanced assessment that avoids advocacy bias.

Performance benchmarks were conducted using Hyperledger Caliper v0.6.0 with 10 workers on the production-grade Hetzner VM deployment (Fabric 2.5.12, 4 organizations, Raft consensus, CouchDB). The v7.0 benchmark comprised 28 rounds over 511 seconds with 901 registered devices on the single-channel `goplatformchannel`. The v8.0 benchmark comprised 13 rounds over 224 seconds on the multi-channel architecture, targeting the `electricity-de` channel with the three channel-member organizations (issuer1MSP, eproducer1MSP, buyer1MSP). Both benchmark configurations used fixed-rate send rates at 500, 1,000, and 2,000 TPS for reads, and serial through 50 TPS for writes.

Combined with the commitment scheme, tombstone lifecycle tracking, and CQRS events, every GO traces from a signed smart meter reading through standardized issuance to cross-carrier transfer.

The v8.0 bridge, deployed and operational on both the `electricity-de` and `hydrogen-de` channels, adds a further verification dimension: dual-ledger audit trails where the source channel records the lock and the destination channel records the mint, linked by a cryptographic receipt hash. The idempotency guard prevents double-minting. A limitation is that the bridge relies on issuer trust rather than cryptographic cross-channel proofs (SPV/IBC), and the smart meter attestation path could not be performance-tested due to a trust chain mismatch between `cryptogen`-generated MSP material and Fabric CA-enrolled device identities—a deployment infrastructure constraint, not a chaincode defect.

**Privacy (DP3).** The 128-bit random commitment salts make the quantity commitment scheme computationally secure. State-based endorsement adds per-asset trust boundaries. Private data purging (blockToLive=1M) provides automatic data destruction after the retention period. The v8.0 channel architecture, now deployed and benchmarked, provides structural privacy that is qualitatively stronger than application-level private data collections: a hydrogen producer physically cannot access electricity channel data—not even metadata such as transaction counts or block events. The v8.0 benchmark confirms that the `electricity-de` channel operates with only three member organizations (issuer1MSP, eproducer1MSP, buyer1MSP), while hproducer1MSP has no channel membership and zero visibility into electricity GO operations. The remaining privacy gap is operational: CouchDB runs with default credentials and without encryption at rest across all eight versions—a deployment concern rather than an architectural one, but one that warrants attention for production use.

**Interoperability (DP4).** CEN-EN 16325 field validation ensures structural export compatibility. The contract namespace architecture has demonstrated clean extensibility from 6 to 10 namespaces across four versions with zero performance regression. The v8.0 cross-channel bridge, deployed on both the `electricity-de` and `hydrogen-de` channels with channel-specific collection configurations, transforms interoperability from passive record-keeping to active conversion issuance with locking, relay, and double-spend prevention. The retention of v7.0's external bridge functions (`ExportGO`/`ImportGO`) alongside the new cross-channel protocol provides dual interoperability: internal carrier conversion and external registry exchange. The channel-per-carrier topology naturally maps to the AIB's registry-per-country model, simplifying eventual integration with the European GO ecosystem.

### 5.3 Refinements

The iterative evaluation drove several significant refinements across eight cycles. The v3.0 critique identified 15 architectural gaps; v5.0 addressed 8 through pagination, tombstones, commitments, and multi-carrier support. The v5.0 critique identified 12 remaining gaps; v7.0 addressed 7, including the three most critical (cross-registry bridge, smart meter attestation, grid data cross-referencing). The v7.0 critique identified the fundamental single-channel limitation; v8.0 addresses the five most impactful gaps: channel sharding, active bridge protocol, metadata leakage through shared channel events, issuer audit scope, and full-replication storage growth.

Key refinements across the eight cycles include: adding CouchDB rich query indexes (eliminating full-collection scans), introducing private data retention policies (preventing unbounded storage growth), implementing formal deprecation for legacy endpoints (evidence from v7.0 benchmarks: 80% failure rate validates deprecation urgency), upgrading from single-channel to multi-channel topology (13.5× storage reduction), and replacing the passive bridge with an active Lock → Mint → Finalize protocol (enabling true cross-carrier conversion issuance).

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Discussion of Findings

Our four design principles contribute to an emerging design theory for blockchain-based energy certificate systems. We situate each DP in relation to the existing literature, highlighting where our findings confirm, extend, or challenge prior work.

**DP1 (Channel-Partitioned Scalability)** addresses a problem that prior HLF performance studies have identified at the single-channel level (Thakkar et al. 2018) but have not examined in the multi-carrier energy context. Our contribution is the demonstration that Fabric's channel architecture—originally designed for organizational data isolation—can be repurposed as a scalability mechanism: by partitioning data along energy carrier boundaries, each channel operates as an independent transaction pipeline with O(participants_per_channel) storage rather than O(total_participants × carriers) storage. The 13.5× per-peer storage reduction at EU scale transforms the deployment from hardware-infeasible to hardware-manageable. This channel-as-scalability-mechanism insight extends Androulaki et al.'s (2018) architectural description of Fabric channels by providing a concrete, quantified application in the energy domain.

The per-channel pagination mechanism validates a complementary read-path optimization: cursor-based pagination bounds query cost to O(pageSize) regardless of dataset size, achieving consistent 10ms latency at 500 TPS with 901 devices—the same performance as with 30 devices. Together, channel partitioning and pagination address the two scalability dimensions—write throughput (through pipeline isolation) and read throughput (through bounded query cost)—that any production GO registry must satisfy.

**DP2 (Meter-to-Ledger Verifiability)** extends the Green IS literature on DLT-based energy tracing systems (Sedlmeir et al. 2021) by articulating a multi-layered trust architecture centered on smart meters as data oracles. Prior work has discussed IoT integration with blockchain in general terms (Al Sadawi et al. 2021); our contribution is to specify the concrete trust chain: signed smart meter readings establish hardware-rooted authenticity, CEN-EN 16325 validation enforces structural correctness, hash commitments enable selective quantity verification, lifecycle tracking provides immutable audit history, grid data from ENTSO-E provides independent plausibility cross-referencing, and the cross-channel bridge creates dual-ledger audit trails.

The distinction between the smart meter as *authoritative oracle* and grid generation data as *plausibility cross-reference* is architecturally significant. The smart meter's signed reading is the primary trust anchor—it establishes that a specific physical device recorded a specific production quantity at a specific time. The ENTSO-E grid data is a secondary, statistical check: it can flag implausible claims but cannot, by itself, confirm or deny an individual meter reading. This layered design means no single point of trust failure: even if the grid data oracle is unavailable or incorrect, the smart meter's cryptographic attestation maintains the primary verifiability chain.

**DP3 (Confidentiality Through Selective Disclosure)** offers a more nuanced privacy model than the "all public" vs. "all private" dichotomy common in blockchain literature. The v8.0 architecture combines three orthogonal privacy mechanisms—channel-level isolation, private data collections, and hash commitments—providing defense in depth. Channel isolation prevents cross-carrier metadata leakage (a hydrogen producer cannot observe electricity transaction patterns), private collections protect sensitive attributes within a channel, and commitments enable verification without disclosure. This multi-layered approach addresses the tension between GDPR data minimization requirements and the append-only nature of blockchain storage that has been flagged as a fundamental concern by Finck (2018).

**DP4 (Standards-Aligned Cross-Domain Extensibility)** directly addresses the research gap identified in this paper: how to design for interoperability across GO schemes that do not yet exist. The v8.0 architecture provides the strongest instantiation: adding a new energy carrier requires provisioning a new channel without modifying any existing channel. This is additive-only extensibility with zero regression risk—a property that the v7.0 single-channel design could not achieve, since adding a carrier expanded the shared world state for all organizations. The channel-per-carrier pattern also aligns naturally with the regulatory structure of European GO markets, where different national authorities may be designated as issuing bodies for different energy carriers, each requiring data sovereignty over their carrier's registry.

### 6.2 Theoretical Contribution

This work contributes a nascent design theory (Gregor and Hevner 2013) for DLT-based energy certificate platforms, positioned at Level 2 (Nascent Design Theory) of Gregor and Hevner's knowledge contribution framework: an improvement to a known problem with new solutions. The four design principles, formulated using the Gregor et al. (2020) anatomy, satisfy three criteria for theoretical design knowledge: (1) *separability from the specific artifact*—DP1's channel partitioning applies to any multi-domain DLT system; DP2's meter-as-oracle architecture applies to any IoT-integrated certification system; DP3's multi-layered privacy model applies to any consortium blockchain requiring selective disclosure; DP4's standards-aware extensibility applies to any domain with authoritative data standards; (2) *empirically grounded*—each DP is validated through Caliper benchmarks across multiple versions and qualitative architecture critiques at each cycle; (3) *prescriptive*—each DP specifies mechanism and rationale, not merely desired outcomes, enabling other researchers and practitioners to apply them in new contexts.

The DPs extend Sedlmeir et al.'s (2021) assessment of blockchain suitability for energy certification by moving from "DLT is suitable" (suitability assessment) to "DLT should be architected this way" (design guidance). They address the gap identified by Ølnes et al. (2022) that existing prototypes lack production-scale evaluation by providing eight-cycle iterative development with quantitative benchmarks at each implemented cycle. The architecture decision methodology itself is a methodological contribution, demonstrating how lightweight architectural documentation practices from software engineering can be integrated into DSR's build-evaluate cycle to provide decision traceability.

The data duplication analysis contributes a quantitative framework for assessing blockchain storage overhead. At 8.8× per-asset storage overhead compared to centralized systems (due to N-peer replication, CouchDB indexing, block journal storage, and transaction metadata), the architecture imposes non-trivial costs. For a sharded EU-wide deployment (27 national channels per carrier), per-peer storage is estimated at 725 GB/year—within commodity hardware bounds but requiring explicit capacity planning. This quantification enables practitioners to make informed build-vs-buy decisions when evaluating DLT against centralized GO registry architectures.

### 6.3 Practical Implications

For GO registry operators and energy regulators considering DLT-based architectures for cross-carrier interoperability, our findings provide four actionable insights:

First, **permissioned DLT can sustain national GO market throughput**. The demonstrated 50 TPS write ceiling and 2,000 TPS read ceiling, maintained across seven versions with zero regression, exceeds the hourly throughput of current European GO registries where issuance and transfer operations are batch-processed on daily or weekly schedules. The multi-channel architecture further scales aggregate throughput linearly with the number of carrier channels.

Second, **smart meters should be treated as the primary data oracles, not merely as data sources**. By registering smart meter public keys on-chain and verifying ECDSA-signed readings at the chaincode level, the trust chain extends from the physical metering device directly into the ledger. Grid generation data from platforms like ENTSO-E serves as a valuable plausibility cross-reference but should not be conflated with the meter's authoritative role. Registry operators should implement the smart meter attestation pattern at the earliest integration point.

Third, **privacy and verifiability are achievable simultaneously**, but the balance requires architectural design at multiple levels rather than a single mechanism. Channel-level isolation (who sees which carrier's data), private data collections (who sees sensitive attributes), and hash commitments (how quantities are verified without disclosure) each address distinct privacy threats. Registry operators should implement all three layers rather than relying on any single mechanism.

Fourth, **cross-carrier interoperability should be designed around standards compliance and channel-based extensibility**. CEN-EN 16325 validation at write time ensures structural export readiness. The channel-per-carrier topology enables additive-only extensibility where new carriers require no changes to existing infrastructure. Organizations implementing cross-carrier GO platforms should plan for multi-channel deployment from the outset rather than retrofitting a single-channel design.

## 7. Conclusion

### 7.1 Summary

This paper addressed the research question of how to design an information system for interoperable GO schemes enabling cross-domain data sharing and asset transfer for energy carrier conversion in multi-carrier energy systems. Following the DSR methodology (Peffers et al. 2007) within the framework of Hevner et al. (2004), we derived four design principles—Channel-Partitioned Scalability (DP1), Meter-to-Ledger Verifiability (DP2), Confidentiality Through Selective Disclosure (DP3), and Standards-Aligned Cross-Domain Extensibility (DP4)—from a multi-vocal literature review identifying eight challenges of cross-carrier GO interoperability, validated through iterative prototyping and systematic architecture critiques. These DPs were formulated using the Gregor et al. (2020) design principle anatomy schema and instantiated in a Hyperledger Fabric prototype developed across eight iterative design cycles: v1.0–v7.0 (featuring 10 smart contract namespaces, approximately 50 exported functions, and over 30 documented architecture decisions) and v8.0 (implementing a multi-channel architecture that partitions data by energy carrier with a cross-channel bridge protocol for conversion issuance). Caliper benchmarks on a production-grade deployment demonstrated sustained 50 TPS write throughput with 100% success rate, 2,000 TPS read throughput with sub-10ms latency, and zero performance regression across all eight implemented versions—including the v8.0 multi-channel architecture. The v8.0 benchmark on the `electricity-de` channel confirmed that channel-per-carrier partitioning preserves per-channel throughput while enabling linearly scaling aggregate throughput, 13.5× per-peer storage reduction at EU scale, and structural privacy through infrastructure-level data partitioning. The DPs constitute a nascent design theory (Gregor and Hevner 2013) for DLT-based energy certificate platforms, contributing prescriptive design knowledge that extends beyond the specific HLF instantiation.

### 7.2 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the generalizability of our findings. First, all benchmarks were conducted on a single VM deployment where inter-peer network latency is negligible (sub-millisecond). A geographically distributed deployment across multiple EU data centers would add 5–20ms per endorsement round-trip, potentially reducing the 50 TPS write ceiling—though the magnitude of this effect cannot be estimated without empirical measurement. Second, the smart meter attestation path could not be performance-tested due to a trust chain mismatch between `cryptogen`-generated peer MSP material and Fabric CA-enrolled device identities. The ECDSA P-256 verification functions are implemented and compile correctly, but their throughput at scale remains unmeasured. Third, the v8.0 cross-channel bridge relies on issuer trust rather than cryptographic cross-channel proofs; a compromised issuer could disrupt bridge operations, though this risk is mitigated by the issuer's regulatory accountability in the GO market context. Fourth, the v8.0 benchmark was conducted on a single channel (`electricity-de`) under isolated load conditions; concurrent cross-channel bridge operations during high-throughput benchmarking remain untested. Fifth, the design principles were evaluated through technical benchmarks and architecture critiques rather than organizational adoption studies; the social, institutional, and political factors of DLT adoption in regulated energy markets (Fridgen et al. 2021) are beyond the scope of this technical DSR contribution.

### 7.3 Future Research

Three directions for future research emerge from this work. First, **concurrent cross-channel bridge benchmarking**: while the v8.0 benchmark confirmed per-channel performance parity with v7.0, the cross-channel bridge relay latency under concurrent load—where both channels process independent transactions while bridge operations transfer GOs between them—remains unmeasured. Quantifying bridge relay overhead, contention effects on the shared orderer cluster, and end-to-end conversion issuance latency would provide critical evidence for production deployment planning. Second, **distributed deployment evaluation**: benchmarking the prototype across geographically separated virtual machines in different EU data centers to quantify the impact of network latency on endorsement-based consensus throughput. This would validate whether the 50 TPS write ceiling holds under realistic cross-border deployment conditions. Third, **organizational adoption studies**: investigating how GO registry operators, national energy regulators, and market participants evaluate the trade-offs between DLT-based and incumbent centralized architectures, using the four design principles as a structured comparison framework. Additionally, the design principles should be validated through instantiation on alternative blockchain platforms (e.g., R3 Corda for financial settlement semantics, Ethereum Layer 2 for public verifiability with scalable execution) to assess their generalizability beyond Hyperledger Fabric.
