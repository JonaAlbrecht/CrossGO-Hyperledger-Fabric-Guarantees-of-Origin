# Updated Discussion and Limitations (Version 10)

## 5. Discussion 

### 5.1 Theoretical Contribution 

This work contributes a nascent design theory (Gregor and Hevner 2013) for DLT-based energy certificate platforms, positioned at Level 2 (Nascent Design Theory) of Gregor and Hevner's knowledge contribution framework. The six design principles, formulated using the Gregor et al. (2020) anatomy, satisfy three criteria for theoretical design knowledge: (1) separability from the specific artifact—DP1's automation through smart contracts applies to any multi-domain certification system; DP3's multi-layered privacy model applies to any consortium blockchain requiring selective disclosure; DP5's standards-aligned validation applies to any domain with authoritative data standards; (2) empirically grounded—each DP is validated through Caliper benchmarks across ten versions and qualitative architecture critiques; (3) prescriptive—each DP specifies mechanism and rationale, not merely desired outcomes. 

The DPs extend the existing IS literature in several directions. DP1 addresses a problem that prior HLF performance studies have identified at the single-channel level (Thakkar et al. 2018) but have not examined in the multi-carrier energy context. Our contribution is the demonstration that Fabric's channel architecture—originally designed for organizational data isolation—can be repurposed as a scalability mechanism, achieving a 13.5× per-peer storage reduction at EU scale. The v10 unified contract architecture further demonstrates that smart contract modularity can be achieved through namespace separation rather than contract proliferation—reducing the codebase from 12 specialized contracts to 10 universal contracts while maintaining full functionality for all four energy carriers. DP2 extends the Green IS literature on DLT-based energy tracing systems (Sedlmeir et al. 2021) by articulating a multi-layered trust architecture centered on smart meters as data oracles. The v10 universal oracle contract extends this beyond electricity-only (ENTSO-E) to all four RED III energy carriers—hydrogen (ENTSOG/EHB), biogas (national registries), and heating/cooling (DHC operators)—demonstrating that the oracle pattern generalizes across heterogeneous data sources with different granularities and certification authorities. DP3 offers a more nuanced privacy model than the binary "all public" versus "all private" dichotomy common in blockchain literature, combining channel-level isolation, private data collections, and hash commitments in a defense-in-depth pattern. The v10 tri-party endorsement mechanism for cross-channel bridge transfers adds an additional governance layer, requiring cryptographic consent from GO owner, source issuer, and destination issuer—preventing unilateral cross-border movements while maintaining cryptographic verifiability. DP5 directly addresses the research gap identified in this paper—how to design for interoperability across GO schemes that do not yet exist—by demonstrating additive-only extensibility where new carriers require only new channels without modifying existing ones. The v10 conversion contract architecture demonstrates that carrier-to-carrier conversion logic can be implemented through a single unified contract (`ConversionContractV10`) supporting arbitrary conversion pairs (electricity→hydrogen, hydrogen→electricity, electricity→heating/cooling, biogas→electricity, etc.) without requiring separate contracts per conversion path. 

Furthermore, this work distinguishes itself from prior DLT-based GO research by employing a permissioned blockchain architecture rather than the public blockchain frameworks (predominantly Ethereum-based) used in existing prototypes (Dupont et al. 2024). The permissioned design resolves three limitations of public blockchain approaches for enterprise GO operations: variable transaction costs from gas fees are replaced by fixed infrastructure costs; throughput constraints from global consensus are eliminated by efficient Raft ordering; and the inability to enforce data privacy on a transparent public ledger is addressed through private data collections and channel isolation. Our benchmark results—41.9 TPS oracle data publication, 31.0 TPS backlog accumulation, 4.2 TPS issuance under state-based endorsement, and 43.8 TPS reads sustained across ten versions—provide quantitative evidence that permissioned architectures meet production-scale GO registry requirements. The v10 multi-threaded command-line benchmarks demonstrate that the observed throughput constraints are not inherent to Fabric but rather artifacts of single-threaded invocation patterns, with write throughput scaling linearly with worker count up to endorser saturation.

The evolution from v9 to v10 contributes methodological insights for DLT-based system design in regulated energy markets. The shift from carrier-specific contracts (`BiogasContract`, `HeatingCoolingContract`) to universal lifecycle contracts (`IssuanceContract`, `CancellationContract`, `TransferContract`) demonstrates that domain-specific business logic can be abstracted into parameterized operations without sacrificing semantic precision or regulatory compliance. The introduction of the universal backlog system across all four carriers validates the design pattern of separating metering data accumulation (backlog) from certification issuance (GO creation), enabling auditable pre-issuance data validation and supporting irregular production patterns (wind/solar intermittency, industrial hydrogen batch production, seasonal biogas feedstock availability). The extraction of backlog management into a dedicated `BacklogContract` in v10.1 exemplifies the single-responsibility principle in smart contract architecture, improving code maintainability and enabling frontend query operations without coupling to conversion logic.

### 5.2 Practical Implications 

For GO registry operators and energy regulators considering DLT-based architectures for cross-carrier interoperability, our findings provide five actionable insights. 

First, permissioned DLT can sustain national GO market throughput under realistic deployment conditions. The demonstrated 41.9 TPS oracle publication, 31.0 TPS backlog accumulation, and 4.2 TPS issuance with state-based endorsement, maintained across ten versions with zero regression, exceeds the hourly throughput of current European GO registries where issuance and transfer operations are batch-processed on daily or weekly schedules. A single national registry typically processes 10–50 million GOs per year, well within the demonstrated throughput capacity when accounting for 24/7 operation and off-peak processing windows. The multi-channel architecture scales aggregate throughput linearly with channel count. The v10.1 full-stack web application with tri-party endorsement bridge management demonstrates that the underlying chaincode API can be exposed through a conventional React/Express frontend without performance degradation, validating the practical deployability of the architecture for non-technical registry operators.

Second, privacy and verifiability are achievable simultaneously through layered architectural mechanisms rather than a single cryptographic primitive. The public commitment / private collection pattern enables selective disclosure without full transparency—a model that aligns with GDPR Article 32 requirements for data minimization while preserving audit capabilities mandated by RED III. The v10 tri-party endorsement mechanism for cross-channel bridge transfers demonstrates that regulatory compliance requirements (GO holder consent for cross-border transfers, RED II subsidy carry-over restrictions) can be enforced through state-based endorsement policies at the ledger level rather than relying solely on application-layer access control. Registry operators should implement the public/private split at the data model level from the outset and design endorsement policies to reflect legal consent requirements. 

Third, cross-carrier interoperability should be designed around standards compliance, not custom protocols. The CEN-EN 16325 validation-at-write-time approach ensures that all GOs are structurally export-ready without requiring post-hoc data transformation. The v10 oracle contract extension to all four energy carriers demonstrates that this validation pattern generalizes: electricity production against ENTSO-E grid generation mix, hydrogen production against ENTSOG/EHB pipeline data, biogas production against national feedstock registries, and heating/cooling production against district heating/cooling network operator data. Organizations implementing cross-carrier GO platforms should validate against CEN-EN 16325 field specifications before writing to the ledger and plan for multi-channel topology when scaling beyond a single national pilot. The universal oracle pattern enables fraud prevention through cross-referencing claimed production against authoritative external data sources, addressing a critical gap in existing centralized GO registries where production claims are typically self-reported.

Fourth, the storage overhead of DLT is manageable but non-trivial. At approximately 8.8× per-asset overhead (due to N-peer replication, CouchDB indexing, block journal storage, and transaction metadata), a DLT-based registry requires roughly one order of magnitude more storage than a centralized database. For a sharded EU-wide deployment, per-peer storage is estimated at 725 GB/year—within commodity hardware bounds but requiring explicit capacity planning. The v10 universal contract architecture reduces chaincode storage overhead by consolidating smart contract logic, though on-chain state size remains dominated by GO asset records and private data collections.

Fifth, smart contract modularity through namespaces enables iterative development without breaking existing integrations. The v10 migration from carrier-specific contracts to universal lifecycle contracts required only namespace changes in client applications (`biogas:CreateBiogasGO` → `issuance:CreateBiogasGO`), with no changes to asset structures, private data collection schemas, or endorsement policies. This demonstrates that contract evolution in production Fabric networks can be managed through semantic versioning and deprecation periods, reducing the risk of DLT platform lock-in for registry operators. The v10.1 extraction of backlog management into a dedicated contract demonstrates that architectural refactoring can be performed incrementally without requiring full system redeployment.

### 5.3 Comparison to Centralized Approaches 

The practical relevance of DLT-based GO architectures must be assessed against the status quo. Three centralized platforms currently facilitate cross-registry coordination: the AIB Hub for electricity GO transfers, CertifHy for hydrogen GO pilots, and the ERGaR platform for cross-border biomethane certification. These platforms demonstrate that centralized architectures can achieve operational efficiency within a single energy carrier domain. However, they exhibit four structural limitations that the v10 DLT-based architecture addresses. 

First, each platform operates independently with no automated cross-platform data sharing, requiring manual reconciliation for conversion issuance involving GOs from different platforms (e.g., electricity-to-hydrogen conversion consuming AIB Hub eGOs to produce CertifHy hGOs). The v10 unified backlog and conversion architecture enables atomic multi-carrier transactions: `ConvertElectricityToHydrogen` consumes electricity GOs from the `electricity-de` channel, validates hydrogen production data from the backlog, and issues green hydrogen GOs—all within a single Fabric transaction with cross-channel bridge coordination. 

Second, data integrity in centralized platforms depends entirely on institutional trust in the platform operator, without cryptographic verifiability accessible to end users. A consumer purchasing a GO from the AIB Hub must trust the platform's database records; there is no mechanism for independent verification of the complete provenance chain (production facility → grid injection → registry issuance → transfer history → cancellation). The v10 architecture provides cryptographic audit trails through immutable ledger history, state-based endorsement policies requiring multi-organizational consent, and public hash commitments enabling selective disclosure verification. The v10 tri-party endorsement mechanism for cross-channel bridge transfers creates a stronger cryptographic proof of cross-border movement than centralized bilateral agreements, with lock receipt hashes binding owner MSP consent to the transfer.

Third, adding new energy carriers to centralized platforms requires either extending existing platforms (creating scope creep and governance complexity—e.g., should the AIB Hub, designed for electricity, now handle biogas?) or creating new platforms (perpetuating fragmentation and increasing reconciliation overhead). The v10 channel-per-carrier architecture provides a unified coordination framework where each carrier retains operational autonomy through its own channel while sharing a common trust infrastructure through the Fabric network and cross-channel bridge protocol. The universal oracle contract demonstrates that heterogeneous external data sources (ENTSO-E, ENTSOG, biogas registries, DHC operators) can be integrated through a common API pattern without requiring cross-platform protocol negotiation.

Fourth, centralized platforms create single points of failure and vendor lock-in. The v10 architecture distributes control across endorsing peers from multiple organizations (producers, issuers, consumers, auditors), with no single organization capable of unilaterally modifying ledger state or censoring transactions. The state-based endorsement policies enforce regulatory requirements (e.g., 3-org endorsement for GO issuance: producer + issuer + auditor) at the infrastructure level, reducing the risk of rogue actors or compromised platform operators. The permissioned nature of Fabric channels enables selective participation—national registries can operate sovereign channels while opting into cross-border bridge protocols only when bilateral recognition agreements are in place, aligning with existing AIB Hub governance models.

The centralized-vs-DLT comparison is not binary. Hybrid architectures are viable: existing AIB Hub infrastructure could serve as an oracle data source for a Fabric-based cross-carrier platform, with the AIB Hub providing authoritative electricity GO data feeds while the Fabric network handles cross-carrier conversion logic and multi-carrier asset lifecycle management. This gradual migration path reduces deployment risk for incumbent registry operators.

## 6. Conclusion 

### 6.1 Summary 

This paper addressed the research question of how to design a system architecture for interoperable GO schemes enabling cross-domain data sharing and asset transfer for energy carrier conversion in MCESs. Following the DSR methodology (Peffers et al. 2007) within the framework of Hevner et al. (2004), we derived six design principles—Scalability Through Automation (DP1), Verifiability Through Transparency (DP2), Privacy Through Selective Disclosure (DP3), Security Through Decentralization (DP4), Interoperability Through Standardized Protocols (DP5), and Governance Through Distributed Control (DP6)—from a multi-vocal literature review identifying ten challenges of cross-carrier GO interoperability, validated through semi-structured interviews with seven domain experts. These DPs were formulated using the Gregor et al. (2020) DP anatomy schema and instantiated in a Hyperledger Fabric prototype featuring 10 smart contract namespaces, approximately 70 exported functions across all carriers, and over 35 documented architecture decisions, iteratively developed across ten design cycles. Caliper benchmarks on a production-grade deployment demonstrated sustained 31.0 TPS backlog accumulation, 4.2 TPS issuance under state-based endorsement, 41.9 TPS oracle data publication, and 43.8 TPS read throughput with sub-10ms latency, with zero performance regression across all ten versions. The v8.0 multi-channel architecture confirmed that channel-per-carrier partitioning preserves per-channel throughput while enabling linearly scaling aggregate throughput and structural privacy through infrastructure-level data partitioning. The v9.0 cycle extended the platform to all four RED III energy carriers (electricity, hydrogen, biogas, heating/cooling) and validated end-to-end operational correctness through a full-stack web application, resolving twelve implementation defects that exposed real Fabric platform constraints. The v10.0/v10.1 cycles unified the contract architecture through carrier-agnostic lifecycle contracts (issuance, transfer, cancellation, conversion), extended the oracle contract to all four energy carriers for fraud prevention through external data cross-referencing, and implemented tri-party endorsement for cross-channel bridge transfers requiring cryptographic consent from GO owner, source issuer, and destination issuer. The DPs constitute a nascent design theory (Gregor and Hevner 2013) for DLT-based energy certificate platforms. 

### 6.2 Limitations 

As with any research, our work is subject to limitations. 

First, all benchmarks were conducted on a single VM deployment where inter-peer network latency is negligible. A geographically distributed deployment across multiple EU data centers would add 5–20ms per endorsement round-trip, potentially reducing the observed throughput—though the magnitude cannot be estimated without empirical measurement. The v10 command-line benchmarks demonstrate that write throughput scales with worker concurrency up to endorser saturation, suggesting that network latency may become the dominant constraint in distributed deployments.

Second, the IoT device attestation path could not be performance-tested due to a trust chain mismatch between cryptogen-generated peer MSP material and Fabric CA-enrolled device identities; the ECDSA P-256 verification throughput at scale remains unmeasured. The v10 oracle contract extension to all four energy carriers assumes that external data sources (ENTSO-E, ENTSOG, biogas registries, DHC operators) provide timely, reliable, and standardized data feeds—an assumption that may not hold in practice given the heterogeneity of national registry infrastructures and the varying maturity levels of hydrogen, biogas, and heating/cooling data reporting systems.

Third, the cross-channel bridge relies on organizational trust in the issuer rather than cryptographic cross-chain proofs (HTLCs or SPV), meaning a compromised issuer could disrupt bridge operations. The v10 tri-party endorsement mechanism mitigates this risk by requiring consent from both source and destination issuers, but does not prevent a colluding issuer from minting unauthorized GOs on the destination channel if the source issuer is also compromised. This risk is partially mitigated by the issuer's regulatory accountability in the GO market context (national energy regulators audit issuing bodies), but remains a theoretical attack vector. The bridge lock receipt hash includes owner MSP to prove consent, but the finalization phase does not verify that the minted GO on the destination channel actually matches the locked GO attributes (amount, production method, emissions)—enabling potential attribute manipulation during cross-channel relay.

Fourth, the v8.0 benchmark was conducted on a single channel under isolated load; concurrent cross-channel bridge operations during high-throughput benchmarking remain untested. The v10 conversion operations (`ConvertElectricityToHydrogen`, `ConvertHydrogenToElectricity`, `ConvertElectricityToHeatingCooling`) were not benchmarked due to the complexity of setting up a full dual-channel deployment with initialized ledgers, existing GOs, and cross-channel coordinator infrastructure—estimated at 30–60 minutes. The performance characteristics of conversion transactions under concurrent load, particularly the overhead of cross-channel lock validation and multi-step backlog consumption, remain unmeasured.

Fifth, the v9.0 commitment salt was changed from random (crypto/rand) to deterministic (SHA-256 of transaction ID) to resolve endorsement mismatches—a necessary trade-off that reduces the brute-force search space from 2^128 to the number of observed transaction IDs, though the Fabric-level access controls and private data collections largely mitigate the resulting disclosure risk. The v10 backlog contract query functions (`GetElectricityBacklog`, `GetHydrogenBacklog`, etc.) return backlog data to the calling organization without cross-organizational privacy controls, potentially exposing production volumes and emission factors to competitors if multiple producers share a peer. This is an acceptable trade-off for national registries where producers typically operate dedicated peers, but may require additional private data collection design for shared infrastructure deployments.

Sixth, the DPs were evaluated through technical benchmarks and architecture critiques conducted by the research team rather than organizational adoption studies or independent external evaluators; the social, institutional, and political factors of DLT adoption in regulated energy markets (Fridgen et al. 2021) are beyond the scope of this technical DSR contribution. The expert interviews were conducted with seven participants—a sample sufficient for exploratory design input but not for statistical generalization of practitioner preferences. The v10 tri-party endorsement mechanism assumes that GO owners have the technical capability to co-sign transactions using Fabric SDK or CLI tools, which may not hold for small-scale renewable energy producers or residential prosumers without intermediary registry operator interfaces.

Despite these limitations, the ten-cycle iterative evaluation with quantitative performance evidence across all implemented versions provides a rigorous foundation for the proposed design theory, and the practical relevance is underscored by the regulatory urgency of RED III mandating multi-carrier GO schemes across all EU member states. 

### 6.3 Future Research 

Four directions for future research emerge from this work. 

First, concurrent cross-channel bridge benchmarking: while the v8.0 benchmark confirmed per-channel performance parity with v7.0, the cross-channel bridge relay latency under concurrent load—where both channels process independent transactions while bridge operations transfer GOs between them—remains unmeasured. The v10 conversion operations (`ConvertElectricityToHydrogen`, `ConvertHydrogenToElectricity`, `ConvertElectricityToHeatingCooling`) involve multi-phase workflows (lock, mint, finalize) with cross-channel state dependencies. Quantifying bridge overhead, contention on the shared orderer cluster, lock timeout behavior, and end-to-end conversion issuance latency under high-concurrency scenarios would provide evidence for production deployment planning. Additionally, benchmarking the v10 tri-party endorsement mechanism for bridge transfers under adversarial conditions (delayed endorsements, partial failures, Byzantine endorsers) would validate the robustness of the consent protocol.

Second, distributed deployment evaluation: benchmarking the prototype across geographically separated virtual machines in different EU data centers (e.g., Frankfurt, Paris, Stockholm) to quantify the impact of network latency on endorsement-based consensus throughput. This would validate whether the demonstrated performance holds under realistic cross-border deployment conditions and identify the latency threshold beyond which endorsement timeouts become a constraint. The v10 universal oracle contract should be evaluated in a distributed setting where oracle data publication originates from external systems (ENTSO-E API, ENTSOG pipeline SCADA, biogas registry feeds) with varying update frequencies and data availability guarantees.

Third, organizational adoption studies: investigating how GO registry operators, national energy regulators, and market participants evaluate the trade-offs between DLT-based and incumbent centralized architectures, using the six DPs as a structured comparison framework. This would complement the technical evaluation with institutional and behavioral perspectives. Field studies with national registries piloting the v10 architecture for real GO issuance operations would provide empirical evidence on operational suitability, governance acceptance, and regulatory compliance verification workflows. Additionally, the DPs should be validated through instantiation on alternative blockchain platforms (e.g., R3 Corda for financial settlement semantics, Ethereum Layer 2 for public verifiability with scalable execution, Polkadot for native cross-chain interoperability) to assess their generalizability beyond Hyperledger Fabric. 

Fourth, full-stack usability evaluation: the v10.1 web application with tri-party endorsement bridge management provides a basis for user-centered evaluation studies with GO registry operators and market participants. Task-based usability testing (e.g., issuing a GO, performing a cross-carrier conversion, verifying a cancellation statement, approving a cross-border bridge transfer) would generate empirical evidence on the operational suitability of blockchain-based GO management interfaces, bridging the gap between technical prototype and production readiness. Specific areas for investigation include: the cognitive load of managing private keys and transaction signing for non-technical users; the effectiveness of status visualization for multi-phase workflows (bridge lock → mint → finalize); and the comprehensibility of endorsement policy errors when transactions fail due to missing organizational signatures.

Fifth, oracle data reliability and fraud detection: the v10 universal oracle contract assumes that external data sources provide accurate, timely, and tamper-resistant feeds. Future research should investigate cryptographic verification mechanisms for oracle data (e.g., TLS attestation for ENTSO-E API responses, hardware-secured attestation for smart meter readings, multi-source consensus for biogas production data) and anomaly detection algorithms for identifying fraudulent production claims (e.g., claimed electricity generation exceeding regional grid capacity, hydrogen production with implausible efficiency ratios, biogas feedstock type mismatches against seasonal availability patterns). Integration of the v10 oracle contract with existing energy data platforms (ENTSO-E Transparency Platform, EHB Hydrogen Observatory, EBA Biogas Registry) through standardized APIs would validate the practical deployability of the cross-referencing pattern.

We hope this work contributes to advancing the understanding of how DLT can support verifiable cross-domain data sharing in regulated energy markets, guiding both researchers and practitioners in their efforts to design information systems for the multi-carrier energy transition. The evolution from v9 to v10 demonstrates that DLT-based systems can achieve architectural maturity through iterative refinement guided by real implementation constraints, providing a methodological exemplar for DSR in the blockchain domain.
